Appraisal Process

Redesigning Valeo's annual employee appraisal system — distinct, intuitive flows for HR, leaders, and managers, replacing a fragmented paper-and-email process with a single digital platform.

01 — Problem Space

A broken appraisal cycle

Valeo's annual performance appraisal ran across paper forms, shared spreadsheets, and email threads. Three distinct user groups — HR, leaders, and managers — had no unified digital workflow, leading to missed deadlines, inconsistent evaluations, and poor visibility into cycle progress.

🗂️ No single platform

HR teams coordinated cycles across Excel exports and email with no live view of submission status or review progress.

🔄 Three roles, three broken flows

HR, leaders, and managers each had different needs yet used the same generic forms, causing confusion and rework at every stage.

📉 Low completion rates

Without reminders, status tracking, or a clear digital home, appraisal cycles regularly ran weeks over schedule.

"How might we design a digital appraisal system that gives each role a clear, tailored workflow — while keeping the entire cycle visible and on track?"

02 — Research

Understanding each role's journey

We interviewed 12 participants across the three roles and mapped their current process end-to-end to identify where breakdowns occurred most frequently.

Role clarity is essential

📋

HR couldn't see who was blocking progress. Clear status indicators could cut follow-up emails by 80%+.

⏰

Deadlines need surfacing

Users only discovered they were late when HR chased them. In-product visibility was critical.

04 — Designs

Three flows — one cohesive system

HR Flow — Cycle management & employee coordination

HR coordinators get a dashboard view of the full appraisal cycle — status indicators eliminate the need for email chasers entirely.

Leader Flow — Team review & rating submission

Leaders see direct reports' self-assessments and submit structured evaluations — guided through each team member with clear rating inputs.

Manager Flow — Calibration, sign-off & final review

Comparative view highlights discrepancies between self-assessment and leader scores, making calibration faster and more consistent.

05 — Design Decisions

Key choices

Before

One generic form for all roles

Actions were unclear, responsibilities overlapped, and users had to figure out what they needed to do themselves.

After

Role-gated entry with tailored flows

HR gets cycle oversight, leaders get review queues, managers get calibration dashboards.

Before

No visibility into cycle progress

HR sent chase emails. Completion rates suffered.

After

Inline real-time status indicators

Every row shows Pending, In Review, Completed, or Overdue — instant visibility, no manual chasing.

07 — Learnings

What this taught me

01

Role clarity is the foundation of enterprise UX

In multi-stakeholder systems, the biggest UX win is often simply making clear who does what and when. Clarity replaces confusion — without needing a single extra feature.

02

Status visibility replaces communication overhead

Every email we eliminated was a design opportunity. A systemic fix, not a feature addition. Designing for transparency reduces the need for coordination.

03

Designing for multiple roles requires discipline

The temptation is one flexible interface. The better answer is three focused ones. Sharing a single system doesn't mean sharing a single screen.

10,000-Floor Elevator

A speculative UX research study exploring how a control interface for a 10,000-floor elevator would work — questioning assumptions around numbering, navigation, wayfinding, and cognitive load at extreme scale.

01 — Problem Space

What happens when scale breaks convention?

Standard elevator UI is designed for a few dozen floors. But what if you had 10,000? This project explores the point at which familiar patterns collapse — and what a truly rethought control interface would need to do differently.

🔢 Numbers become meaningless

Scrolling through 10,000 floor buttons is unusable. The conventional floor-number model breaks down entirely.

🧠 Cognitive overload by default

Without zoning, categorisation, or search, users face a decision space that exceeds human working memory by orders of magnitude.

🗺️ Wayfinding has no reference points

Floors need a new type of identity — not just numbers, but zones, landmarks, and contextual anchors users can reason about.

"How might we design an elevator control interface that allows a person to navigate 10,000 floors quickly, confidently, and without cognitive overload?"

02 — Research

Studying how people orient in large spaces

The research drew on analogies from large-scale wayfinding systems — airports, metro networks, hospital campuses — mapping the heuristics that make them navigable despite their scale.

10K

7±2

🛫

Zones before numbers

Airports navigate by terminal → concourse → gate. A 10,000-floor building needs the same zone hierarchy to make navigation tractable.

🔍

Search over scroll

Users need to describe their destination, not locate it in a list. A search model transfers naturally to extreme-scale navigation.

⭐

Recents & favourites are essential

The vast majority of elevator journeys are repeat trips. Surfacing recent destinations eliminates the full navigation problem for most users.

04 — Key Designs

Rethinking the panel entirely

Design Exploration — Zone-based navigation & search interface

Zone selector + search bar + recents panel — reducing 10,000 choices to a 3-step interaction: pick zone, refine, confirm floor.

05 — Design Decisions

Key choices

Convention

One button per floor

At 10,000 floors, physically impossible to render and cognitively impossible to use. The model collapses entirely at this scale.

Solution

Zone hierarchy + search

Navigate via zones or type/speak destination. Most users never leave the Recents panel — the hard problem solves itself.

Convention

Floor numbers as identity

Number 7,431 has no meaning — users cannot orient themselves. Numbers confirm location, they don't communicate it.

Solution

Named zones as primary navigation

Each zone carries a name (district, function, landmark) as a meaningful anchor. Numbers confirm, not navigate.

07 — Learnings

What this taught me

01

Extreme constraints unlock first-principles thinking

Designing for 10,000 floors forces you to question every assumption. Convention becomes useless — which is where the most interesting design thinking happens.

02

Wayfinding analogies are a powerful research tool

When no precedent exists in your domain, look sideways. Airports and metro systems have solved large-scale navigation — their patterns translate more directly than expected.

03

Most users only need a fraction of the interface

Designing for 10,000 destinations sounds overwhelming. But 80% of journeys are repeat trips. Designing Recents well solves the real-world problem for the majority.

Bus Tracker Mobile App

A full-cycle UX research and design project — uncovering the real frustrations of public transit commuters and translating them into a clear, real-time bus tracking mobile experience.

01 — Problem Space

Commuting by bus shouldn't feel like a guessing game

Public transit riders face a daily uncertainty problem — they have no reliable way of knowing when their bus will arrive, whether it's running late, or which route to take to minimise wait time. The result is wasted time, missed connections, and a growing preference for private transport even when buses are available.

🚌 No real-time visibility

Printed timetables and static route maps give no indication of where a bus actually is or when it will arrive at a specific stop.

😤 High uncertainty = high stress

Commuters arrive early and wait, not knowing if the bus is 2 minutes away or 20. The anxiety of not knowing drives people away from public transit entirely.

🗺️ Route complexity for new users

Finding the right bus, the right stop, and the right connection is unintuitive for infrequent users — there's no single tool that brings it all together.

"How might we design a mobile experience that gives commuters real-time confidence about their bus — so that waiting at a stop feels informed rather than anxious?"

02 — Research

Listening to real commuters

The research phase combined user interviews with observational study at bus stops to understand commuter behaviour, mental models around transit timing, and the coping strategies people use when information is absent.

8
In-depth user interviews
3

72%
Said uncertainty was the #1 pain point
4
Competing apps benchmarked
⏱️

Time is the core currency

Every interviewee measured the quality of their commute in minutes wasted. An extra 5 minutes of confident waiting was valued over 15 minutes of uncertain waiting.

📍

Location context over schedules

Users didn't want to know when the bus was scheduled to arrive — they wanted to know where it was right now and when it would reach their specific stop.

🔄

Routine trips need zero friction

For daily commuters, the app needs to surface the relevant route instantly — not ask them to search every time. Favourite stops and saved routes are essential.

Research artefacts — personas & journey mapping

Three distinct personas emerged: the daily commuter, the occasional rider, and the transit newcomer — each with different frequency, familiarity, and tolerance for complexity.

Affinity mapping across interview notes surfaced clusters around time uncertainty, stop identification difficulty, and the absence of a trusted single source of truth.

Wireframes & information architecture

Low-fidelity wireframes mapped the core flows: finding a nearby stop, tracking an active bus, and saving a favourite route. Three taps maximum from launch to live tracking.

04 — Final Designs

A calm, confident transit experience

The final UI was built around one principle: every screen should reduce anxiety, not add to it. Real-time position, ETA in minutes, and route status at a glance — without requiring the user to search or scroll.

Home & live tracking — real-time bus position

The home screen surfaces the user's saved stops and shows live ETAs without requiring any interaction. One tap reveals the bus position on the map.

Route search & stop detail

Route search returns results ranked by ETA at the user's closest stop, not by route number. Stop detail shows all upcoming buses, their live position, and occupancy level.

Notifications & trip planning

Users set a "heads up" alert — the app notifies them when the bus is N minutes from their stop, so they can leave at the right moment rather than waiting in the cold.

05 — Design Decisions

Key choices

Assumption

Show full route schedules

Initial concept displayed all scheduled departures for a stop — which is what existing apps do. Users found it overwhelming and didn't trust the times.

Decision

Lead with live ETA, not timetables

We replaced schedule lists with a single prominent ETA counter in minutes. Live data only. Schedules moved to a secondary "more info" layer.

Assumption

Map-first navigation

Early wireframes opened directly on a full-screen map. Usability tests showed this was disorienting — users didn't know what stop to tap or why.

Decision

List-first with map on demand

Home screen lists the user's nearby stops with live ETAs. Map is one tap away and opens pre-zoomed to the relevant bus position.

Assumption

Generic search as the primary entry point

Search-heavy onboarding required users to know route numbers or stop names — a barrier for new and infrequent users.

Decision

Location-aware suggestions by default

App opens to stops within 500m of the user's current location, ranked by next departure. No search required for the most common use case.

07 — Learnings

What this taught me

01

Real-time data changes the UX problem entirely

Once you commit to live data, static patterns — lists, timetables, scheduled routes — become the enemy. The design must be built around a moving truth, not a fixed one. That requires rethinking every screen from scratch.

02

Anxiety is a UX problem, not just a UI problem

The core user need wasn't information — it was emotional: to feel in control of their time. That distinction changed how I thought about hierarchy, motion, and data density across every screen.

03

The zero-search experience is worth fighting for

The biggest friction in transit apps is the assumption that users want to search. Most users just want to leave home at the right moment. Designing for location awareness first — and search as a fallback — halved task completion time.
