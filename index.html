Appraisal
Process

Redesigning Valeo's annual employee appraisal system — distinct, intuitive flows for HR, leaders, and managers, replacing a fragmented paper-and-email process with a single digital platform.

Sarah Abouzainah
Valeo Egypt

HR Tech · Performance Mgmt
01 — Problem Space

A broken appraisal cycle

Valeo's annual performance appraisal ran across paper forms, shared spreadsheets, and email threads. Three distinct user groups — HR, leaders, and managers — had no unified digital workflow, leading to missed deadlines, inconsistent evaluations, and poor visibility into cycle progress.

🗂️ No single platform

HR teams coordinated cycles across Excel exports and email with no live view of submission status or review progress.

🔄 Three roles, three broken flows

HR, leaders, and managers each had different needs yet used the same generic forms, causing confusion and rework at every stage.

📉 Low completion rates

Without reminders, status tracking, or a clear digital home, appraisal cycles regularly ran weeks over schedule.

"How might we design a digital appraisal system that gives each role a clear, tailored workflow while keeping the entire cycle visible and on track?"

02 — Research

Understanding each role's journey

We interviewed 12 participants across the three roles and mapped their current process end-to-end to identify where breakdowns occurred most frequently.

12Role-based interviews
3Distinct user roles mapped
67%Said deadline tracking was top pain
5×Avg. email exchanges per review cycle
👥

Role clarity is essential

Each role had entirely different tasks. A single interface caused confusion about who should act next.

📋

Status visibility drives completion

HR couldn't see who was blocking progress. Clear status indicators could cut follow-up emails by 80%+.

⏰

Deadlines need surfacing

Users only discovered they were late when HR chased them. In-product visibility was critical.

04 — Designs

Three flows — one cohesive system

HR Flow
Cycle management & employee coordination
HR coordinators get a dashboard view of the full appraisal cycle — status indicators eliminate the need for email chasers entirely.
Leader Flow
Team review & rating submission
Leaders see direct reports' self-assessments and submit structured evaluations — guided through each team member with clear rating inputs.
Manager Flow
Calibration, sign-off & final review
Comparative view highlights discrepancies between self-assessment and leader scores, making calibration faster and more consistent.
05 — Design Decisions

Key choices

One generic form for all roles

Actions were unclear, responsibilities overlapped, and users had to figure out what they needed to do themselves.

Role-gated entry with tailored flows

HR gets cycle oversight, leaders get review queues, managers get calibration dashboards.

No visibility into cycle progress

HR sent chase emails. Completion rates suffered.

Inline real-time status indicators

Every row shows Pending, In Review, Completed, or Overdue — instant visibility, no manual chasing.

07 — Learnings

What this taught me

01
Role clarity is the foundation of enterprise UX

In multi-stakeholder systems, the biggest UX win is often simply making clear who does what and when.

02
Status visibility replaces communication overhead

Every email we eliminated was a design opportunity. A systemic fix, not a feature addition.

03
Designing for multiple roles requires discipline

The temptation is one flexible interface. The better answer is three focused ones.

10,000-Floor
Elevator

A speculative UX research study exploring how a control interface for a 10,000-floor elevator would work — questioning assumptions around numbering, navigation, wayfinding, and cognitive load at extreme scale.

Speculative DesignUX ResearchInteraction DesignInformation ArchitectureCognitive Load
Sarah Abouzainah
Personal Research Project
2019
Speculative UX · Interaction Design
01 — Problem Space

What happens when scale breaks convention?

Standard elevator UI is designed for a few dozen floors. But what if you had 10,000? This project explores the point at which familiar patterns collapse — and what a truly rethought control interface would need to do differently.

🔢 Numbers become meaningless

Scrolling through 10,000 floor buttons is unusable. The conventional floor-number model breaks down entirely.

🧠 Cognitive overload by default

Without zoning, categorisation, or search, users face a decision space that exceeds human working memory by orders of magnitude.

🗺️ Wayfinding has no reference points

Floors need a new type of identity — not just numbers, but zones, landmarks, and contextual anchors users can reason about.

"How might we design an elevator control interface that allows a person to navigate 10,000 floors quickly, confidently, and without cognitive overload?"

02 — Research

Studying how people orient in large spaces

The research drew on analogies from large-scale wayfinding systems — airports, metro networks, hospital campuses — mapping the heuristics that make them navigable despite their scale.

10KFloors to navigate
7±2Miller's Law — items in working memory
3Wayfinding analogies studied
4Alternative interface concepts
🛫

Zones before numbers

Airports navigate by terminal → concourse → gate. A 10,000-floor building needs the same zone hierarchy.

🔍

Search over scroll

Users need to describe their destination, not locate it in a list. A search model transfers naturally to extreme-scale navigation.

⭐

Recents & favourites are essential

The vast majority of elevator journeys are repeat trips. Surfacing recent destinations eliminates the full navigation problem for most users.

Design Exploration

Zone selector + search bar + recents panel — reducing 10,000 choices to a 3-step interaction: pick zone, refine, confirm floor.
05 — Design Decisions

Key choices

One button per floor

At 10,000 floors, physically impossible to render and cognitively impossible to use.

Zone hierarchy + search

Navigate via zones or type/speak destination. Most users never leave the Recents panel.

Floor numbers as identity

Number 7,431 has no meaning — users cannot orient themselves.

Named zones as primary navigation

Each zone carries a name (district, function, landmark) as a meaningful anchor. Numbers confirm, not navigate.

07 — Learnings

What this taught me

01
Extreme constraints unlock first-principles thinking

Designing for 10,000 floors forces you to question every assumption. Convention becomes useless — which is where the most interesting design thinking happens.

02
Wayfinding analogies are a powerful research tool

When no precedent exists in your domain, look sideways. Airports and metro systems have solved large-scale navigation — their patterns translate more directly than expected.

03
Most users only need a fraction of the interface

Designing for 10,000 destinations sounds overwhelming. But 80% of journeys are repeat trips. Designing Recents well solves the real-world problem for the majority.

Workforce

A real-time workforce capacity management tool for engineering managers — team visibility, smarter project allocation, faster request assessment, and proactive hiring decisions.

01 — Problem Space

Engineering managers are flying blind

Engineering managers had no single place to see their team's capacity. Project allocation, incoming requests, and hiring decisions were handled reactively — through spreadsheets, ad-hoc meetings, and gut feel.

🔎 No capacity visibility

Managers couldn't tell who was overloaded until someone missed a deadline or burned out.

⚡ Reactive to requests

Answering "can we take this?" required 2–3 coordination meetings. No quick feasibility check existed.

📅 Hiring always too late

Without forecasting, talent gaps only became visible when delivery was already at risk.

"How might we give engineering managers a single real-time view of their team's capacity — so they can allocate better, respond to requests in seconds, and hire before it becomes urgent?"

02 — Research

Understanding the manager's daily reality

Contextual interviews with 8 engineering managers and a survey of 40, focused on how they track capacity and what decisions they struggle with most.

8Manager depth interviews
40Survey respondents
74%Used spreadsheets for capacity
3×Avg. meetings per request answer
89%Said they hired too late
📊

Fragmented data

Capacity data lived in HR systems, project trackers, and spreadsheets — never in one actionable place.

🕐

Time is the key axis

Managers needed to view capacity at different horizons — a month view for now, a year view for planning.

🏗️

Hierarchy gives meaning

A flat member list was useless. Capacity only became actionable within the team structure managers actually ran.

04 — Design Decisions

Key choices

Separate Team + Capacity pages

Constant context-switching to answer one question: "Is this person free and what are they working on?"

Merged view with expandable rows

Tree hierarchy shows committed/free/load inline. Expanding ▸ reveals sub-project allocations without leaving the page.

Static single-period view

One timeframe was never enough to make a good planning decision.

Multi-level period navigation

Month / Quarter / Semester / Year. Member Profile shows all four timeframes simultaneously.

Decorative severity pills

Managers had to manually hunt for relevant people.

Severity pills as interactive filters

One click instantly filters the tree to overloaded members across all sub-teams.

06 — Learnings

What this taught me

01
Data-dense ≠ overwhelming — context is everything

The challenge wasn't simplification. It was giving every number its organizational and project context so it was immediately actionable.

02
Time is a first-class design dimension

Designing navigable period hierarchies fundamentally changed how managers could plan ahead.

03
Merge pages when the decision is the same

Merging Team and Capacity removed a mental model split that served the tool, not the user.
