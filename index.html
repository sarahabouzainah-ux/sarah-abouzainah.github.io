Appraisal
Process

Redesigning Valeo's annual employee appraisal system — creating distinct, intuitive flows for HR teams, leaders, and managers, replacing a fragmented paper-and-email process with a single digital platform.

Enterprise HRProcess DesignUX ResearchUI DesignRole-Based FlowsSketch · Miro
Sarah Abouzainah
Valeo Egypt
Sketch · Miro
March 2021
HR Tech · Performance Mgmt
01 — Problem Space

A broken appraisal cycle

Valeo's annual performance appraisal ran across paper forms, shared spreadsheets, and email threads. Three distinct user groups — HR, leaders, and managers — had no unified digital workflow, leading to missed deadlines, inconsistent evaluations, and poor visibility into cycle progress.

🗂️ No single platform

HR teams coordinated cycles across Excel exports and email. There was no live view of who had completed their self-assessment or who was waiting on a manager review.

🔄 Three roles, three broken flows

HR, leaders, and managers each had different needs during the appraisal cycle — yet they all used the same generic forms, causing confusion and rework at every stage.

📉 Low completion rates

Without reminders, status tracking, or a clear digital home for the process, appraisal cycles regularly ran weeks over schedule with incomplete submissions.

"How might we design a digital appraisal system that gives each role — HR, leader, and manager — a clear, tailored workflow while keeping the entire cycle visible and on track?"

02 — Research

Understanding each role's journey

We interviewed 12 participants across the three roles: 4 HR coordinators, 4 team leaders, and 4 managers. We mapped their current process end-to-end and identified where breakdowns occurred most frequently.

12Role-based interviews
3Distinct user roles mapped
67%Said deadline tracking was a top pain
5×Avg. email exchanges per review cycle
👥

Insight 1 — Role clarity is essential

Each role had entirely different tasks: HR coordinates, leaders review teams, managers calibrate. A single shared interface caused confusion about who should act next.

📋

Insight 2 — Status visibility drives completion

HR couldn't see who was blocking progress. Adding clear status indicators could reduce follow-up emails by 80%+.

⏰

Insight 3 — Deadlines need to be surfaced

Users only discovered they were late when HR sent a chase email. In-product deadline visibility and nudges were critical to keeping cycles on track.

04 — Designs

Three flows — one cohesive system

Each role received a distinct, purpose-built flow. The designs below are the final deliverables published on Behance.

HR Flow
Cycle management & employee coordination
HR coordinators get a dashboard view of the full appraisal cycle — who has submitted, who is pending, and where each employee is in the review chain. Status indicators eliminate the need for email chasers.
Leader Flow
Team review & rating submission
Leaders see their direct reports' self-assessments and submit structured evaluations through each team member with clear rating inputs — no blank forms, no ambiguity.
Manager Flow
Calibration, sign-off & final review
Managers review leader assessments, apply calibration adjustments, and sign off on final ratings. The comparative view highlights discrepancies, making calibration decisions faster and more consistent.
05 — Design Decisions

Key choices that shaped the design

One generic form for all roles

Every user — HR, leader, manager — accessed the same undifferentiated form. Actions were unclear, responsibilities overlapped, and users had to figure out what they needed to do themselves.

Role-gated entry with tailored flows

Users enter through their role and see only what's relevant. HR gets cycle oversight, leaders get review queues, managers get calibration dashboards — three distinct, purposeful experiences.

No visibility into cycle progress

HR had to send emails asking for status updates. Employees didn't know if they were blocking the process. Completion rates suffered.

Inline status indicators throughout

Every employee row and review item shows a real-time status — Pending, In Review, Completed, Overdue — giving HR instant cycle visibility without any manual chasing.

07 — Learnings

What this project taught me

01
Role clarity is the foundation of enterprise UX

In multi-stakeholder enterprise systems, the biggest UX win is often simply making clear who does what and when. Role-gated flows reduce cognitive load far more than visual polish.

02
Status visibility replaces communication overhead

Every email we eliminated was a design opportunity. Surfacing real-time cycle status in the UI removed the need for coordination entirely — a systemic fix, not a feature addition.

03
Valeo's brand extends naturally to data-dense UIs

The Valeo green works as a primary action and status color in enterprise interfaces — energetic enough to signal progress, professional enough to stay out of the way.

Workforce

A workforce capacity management tool for engineering managers — giving them real-time team visibility, smarter project allocation, faster request assessment, and proactive hiring decisions.

01 — Problem Space

Engineering managers are flying blind

Engineering managers at Valeo had no single place to see their team's capacity. Project allocation, incoming requests, and hiring decisions were all handled reactively — through spreadsheets, ad-hoc meetings, and gut feel.

🔎 No capacity visibility

Managers couldn't tell who was overloaded or who had free bandwidth until someone missed a deadline or burned out.

⚡ Reactive to requests

Answering "can we take this?" required 2–3 coordination meetings. There was no quick way to assess feasibility against current load.

📅 Hiring always too late

Without forecasting tools, talent gaps only became visible when delivery was already at risk — too late to hire in time.

"How might we give engineering managers a single real-time view of their team's capacity — so they can allocate better, respond to requests in seconds, and hire before it becomes urgent?"

02 — Research

Understanding the manager's daily reality

Contextual interviews with 8 engineering managers and a survey of 40 managers across Valeo's engineering teams, focused on how they track capacity today and what decisions they struggle with most.

8Manager depth interviews
40Survey respondents
74%Used spreadsheets for capacity
3×Avg. meetings per request answer
89%Said they hired too late
📊

Insight 1 — Fragmented data

Capacity data lived in HR systems, project trackers, and personal spreadsheets — never in one place a manager could act from.

🕐

Insight 2 — Time is the key axis

Managers needed to view capacity at different horizons simultaneously — a month view for today, a year view for planning.

🏗️

Insight 3 — Hierarchy gives meaning

A flat member list was useless. Capacity only became actionable within the team structure the manager actually managed.

05 — Key Screens

Five manager workflows, one tool

The screens below show the final design in Valeo's brand colors — teal nav, lime green actions, and a three-tier severity system (green / amber / red) applied consistently across every capacity indicator.

Screen 1
Overview — Daily team briefing
KPI cards + live utilization bar + capacity alerts with severity colors + request queue with feasibility signals — all in one scroll. The Valeo green period selector anchors the time context throughout.
Screen 2

One unified view shows team hierarchy, per-member load, free hours, and project allocations. The ▸/▾ expand toggle connects each member directly to sub-projects — replacing the need for a separate Capacity page.
06 — Design Decisions

Key choices that shaped the design

Separate Team + Capacity pages

Users had to navigate between two pages to answer one question: "Is this person free and what are they working on?" Constant context-switching frustrated managers.

Merged "Team & Capacity" with expandable rows

Tree hierarchy shows committed/free/load inline. Expanding ▸ reveals exact sub-project allocations without leaving the page — hierarchy, capacity, and project context in one view.

Static single-period view

Managers needed to plan across multiple horizons simultaneously — one timeframe was never enough to make a good decision.

Multi-level period navigation + Member Profile cross-view

Month / Quarter / Semester / Year with navigable sub-periods. Member Profile shows all four timeframes simultaneously on one screen.

Decorative severity color pills

Green/amber/red indicators were purely visual. Managers had to manually hunt for relevant people.

Severity pills as interactive filters

Clicking "≥ 90% Critical" instantly filters the tree to show only overloaded members across all sub-teams. One click, zero hunting.

08 — Learnings

What this project taught me

01
Data-dense ≠ overwhelming — context is everything

Engineering managers are comfortable with numbers. The challenge wasn't simplification — it was giving every number its organizational and project context so it was immediately actionable.

02
Time is a first-class design dimension

Most workforce tools collapse time into one view. Designing navigable period hierarchies — and showing cross-period snapshots simultaneously — fundamentally changed how managers could plan ahead.

03
Merge pages when the decision is the same

We started with 6 pages. Testing revealed managers constantly switched between Team and Capacity to answer one question. Merging them removed a mental model split that served the tool, not the user.
